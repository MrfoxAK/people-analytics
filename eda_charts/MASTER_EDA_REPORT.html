1. Class Imbalance — Who Left vs Who Stayed FOUNDATION
📊 What the chart shows
1,233 employees stayed (83.9%) and 237 left (16.1%). The dataset is heavily imbalanced.
🧠 Why this happens
A 16% attrition rate is significantly above the global average of ~10%. This imbalance means a naive model that always predicts "Stay" would be 84% "accurate" — which is useless. This is WHY we used SMOTE in our model.
⚡ Business action / model decision
Used SMOTE oversampling to balance training data 50/50. Chose ROC-AUC and Recall over Accuracy as evaluation metrics. Set decision threshold to 0.35 instead of default 0.5.
02
2. Attrition by Department DEPARTMENT
📊 What the chart shows
Sales has 20.6% attrition, HR has 19%, R&D has 13.8%.
🧠 Why this happens
Sales roles are target-driven, high-pressure, and often have variable pay — leading to burnout and external offers. R&D employees tend to be more engaged with their work and harder to replace, so both sides are more careful.
⚡ Business action / model decision
HR should run separate retention programs per department. Sales needs better commission structures. R&D needs career ladders and research autonomy.
03
3. Overtime — The Single Biggest Driver TOP DRIVER
📊 What the chart shows
Employees doing overtime leave at 30.5% vs 10.4% for those who don't. That is a 3× difference.
🧠 Why this happens
Overtime is a proxy for burnout, poor work-life balance, and understaffing. When a person consistently works beyond contracted hours, the psychological contract with the employer breaks down.
⚡ Business action / model decision
Monitor overtime hours per team. Hire additional headcount where OT is structural. Cap mandatory overtime. Include "overtime rate" as a top feature in the model — it is the single strongest categorical predictor.
04
4. Salary Gap — Leavers Earn $2,046/Month Less SALARY
📊 What the chart shows
Average monthly income of employees who left: $4,787. Those who stayed: $6,833. A $2,046 per month gap.
🧠 Why this happens
Compensation is a hygiene factor — when it feels unfair relative to peers or market, employees leave. The long tail of leavers at very low incomes ($1,000-3,000) shows a "flight-from-the-bottom" pattern.
⚡ Business action / model decision
Conduct market benchmarking annually. Flag employees earning below the 25th percentile ($2,373 for leavers) for immediate salary review. Model uses MonthlyIncome as a top predictive feature.
05
5. Age Distribution — Young Employees Leave Most DEMOGRAPHICS
📊 What the chart shows
Average age of leavers: 33.6 years. Stayers: 37.6 years. The distribution of leavers is skewed toward 25-35.
🧠 Why this happens
Younger employees are in their exploration phase of careers — they have fewer family obligations, more energy to switch, and active LinkedIn networks. They are also more likely to be in lower-paying entry roles.
⚡ Business action / model decision
Targeted retention for employees aged 25-35. Mentorship programs, fast-track promotion pathways, and learning budgets for this cohort.
06
6. Age Group Attrition — 18-25 Group is Critical AGE GROUP
📊 What the chart shows
The 18-25 age group has the highest attrition rate. It drops steadily as age increases.
🧠 Why this happens
This is the "figuring it out" generation — job hopping in early career is normal and even rational. By 40+, employees have mortgages, families, and seniority — switching costs are high.
⚡ Business action / model decision
Graduate hire programs, buddy systems, clear 2-year career roadmaps for new joiners. This group is small in headcount but has disproportionate training cost impact.
07
7. Job Role — Sales Representatives at 40% Attrition ROLE RISK
📊 What the chart shows
Sales Representatives: 39.8%, Laboratory Technicians: 23.9%, HR: 23.1%. Research Directors: only 2.5%.
🧠 Why this happens
Sales Reps have the most commoditised skills, highest market demand, target pressure, and often the widest pay gap between companies. Research Directors are senior, well-compensated, and would lose too much by leaving.
⚡ Business action / model decision
Immediate retention focus on Sales Reps. Investigate whether OTE (on-target earnings) are achievable and fair. Lab Technicians need career progression into Senior/Lead roles.
08
8. Satisfaction Radar — All 4 Dimensions Lower for Leavers SATISFACTION
📊 What the chart shows
Leavers score lower on all 4 satisfaction dimensions: Job (2.47 vs 2.78), Environment (2.46 vs 2.77), Work-Life (2.66 vs 2.78), Relationship (2.60 vs 2.73).
🧠 Why this happens
Satisfaction is cumulative. No single dimension is catastrophically different — but the combination creates a "death by a thousand cuts" effect. Job + Environment satisfaction show the largest gaps.
⚡ Business action / model decision
Run pulse surveys quarterly. Build a composite SatisfactionScore (as we did in feature engineering). Flag employees with average score below 2.5 for manager check-in.
09
9. Marital Status — Singles Leave 2.5× More LIFE STAGE
📊 What the chart shows
Single employees leave at 25.5%, Married at 12.5%, Divorced at 10.1%.
🧠 Why this happens
Single employees have lower financial obligations, more flexibility, and often higher career ambition. They are less "anchored" to geography and more willing to pursue opportunities elsewhere.
⚡ Business action / model decision
Not something to discriminate on — but informs retention strategy. Single employees benefit more from social engagement programs, relocation flexibility, and fast-track opportunities.

10. Stock Options — No Equity = 2.6× Higher Attrition EQUITY
📊 What the chart shows
Level 0 (no stock): 24.4% attrition. Level 1: 9.4%. Level 2: 7.6%. Level 3: 17.6% (interesting dip).
🧠 Why this happens
Stock options create "golden handcuffs." Employees with unvested equity have a financial reason to stay. The spike at Level 3 may indicate senior employees receiving offers that even outweigh their equity.
⚡ Business action / model decision
Extend stock option eligibility to more employees, especially high-risk roles (Sales Reps, Lab Technicians). Stagger vesting over 4 years. This was a top feature in our XGBoost model.
11
11. Business Travel — Frequent Travellers Leave 3× More TRAVEL
📊 What the chart shows
Non-travel: 8%, Rarely: 15%, Frequently: 24.9%. Each step up triples the risk.
🧠 Why this happens
Frequent travel disrupts personal life, family routines, and physical health. It signals a role that lacks work-life balance — especially damaging for single employees or those with young families.
⚡ Business action / model decision
Rebalance travel requirements. Offer travel compensation/perks for frequent travellers. Explore virtual collaboration as substitute. Flag frequent travellers with low WLB scores for intervention.
12
12. Tenure — The First Year is the Most Dangerous CRITICAL RISK
📊 What the chart shows
0-1 year employees have the highest attrition (~35%). It drops sharply after 3+ years. Very long-tenured employees (20+ yrs) stay almost universally.
🧠 Why this happens
The onboarding period is the highest-risk window. If a new hire does not feel connected, productive, and valued in the first 90 days, they exit. This is called the "new employee cliff."
⚡ Business action / model decision
Invest heavily in structured onboarding programs. Assign buddies and clear 30-60-90 day plans. Monitor new hire satisfaction at 30, 60, 90, and 180 days. This is when retention ROI is highest.

13. Job Level — Entry Level (L1) is the Weakest Link SENIORITY
📊 What the chart shows
Job Level 1: 26.3% attrition. Level 2: 9.7%. Level 3: 14.7%. Levels 4-5: <7%. As income rises with level, attrition falls.
🧠 Why this happens
Entry-level employees earn the least, have the least organisational investment in them, and have the most external alternatives. The income-attrition relationship is almost perfectly inverse.
⚡ Business action / model decision
L1 employees need clear promotion timelines (e.g., "Level 2 in 18 months if you hit X"). Regular performance feedback. Mentoring from L3+ seniors.

14. Correlation Heatmap — What Actually Correlates with Attrition? ML INSIGHT
📊 What the chart shows
Attrition_bin correlates negatively with: MonthlyIncome (-0.16), JobLevel (-0.17), TotalWorkingYears (-0.17), YearsAtCompany (-0.13), StockOptionLevel (-0.14). Positively with: OverTime (encoded) and NumCompaniesWorked.
🧠 Why this happens
The correlation values are moderate — attrition is not driven by one variable but by combinations. This is why an ML ensemble outperforms simple rules or single-variable analysis.
⚡ Business action / model decision
Use this to select features for modelling. High multicollinearity (YearsAtCompany ↔ TotalWorkingYears: 0.63) means we should be careful or engineer composite features instead.

16. Promotion Lag — Stagnation Drives Exits CAREER GROWTH
📊 What the chart shows
Employees just promoted: low attrition. 5+ years with no promotion: highest attrition. Each year without promotion increases risk.
🧠 Why this happens
Employees are signalling machines. If 3+ years pass without recognition (promotion/raise), they assume the ceiling has been reached and begin looking externally.
⚡ Business action / model decision
Implement maximum promotion review windows (e.g., mandatory promotion consideration every 2 years). Track "time since last promotion" as an HR leading indicator. This is the YearsSinceLastPromotion feature in our model.

17. Training Investment — 0 Trainings = 25% Attrition L&D
📊 What the chart shows
Employees with 0 training sessions: 25%+ attrition. 3 sessions: ~14%. 6 sessions: lowest attrition.
🧠 Why this happens
Training signals that the employer is investing in the employee's future. Lack of training signals stagnation. It also impacts skill currency — employees who aren't growing feel left behind.
⚡ Business action / model decision
Mandate minimum 2 training sessions per employee per year. Measure "training investment" as an HR KPI. Particularly important for tech roles where skill obsolescence is fast.

18. Gender — Not a Key Attrition Driver FAIRNESS
📊 What the chart shows
Male attrition: ~17%. Female: ~15%. The difference is small and may not be statistically significant.
🧠 Why this happens
Gender alone is not predictive of attrition. The factors driving gender-based attrition differences (if any) are more likely rooted in role distribution, pay equity, and work-life balance policies.
⚡ Business action / model decision
Do not use gender as a predictor in the model (ethical + low signal). Instead, investigate if women are concentrated in high-attrition roles. Ensure pay equity audits are conducted.

19. Education Field — HR Grads Leave Most EDUCATION
📊 What the chart shows
Human Resources education background: highest attrition. Life Sciences and Medical: lower. Technical fields in the middle.
🧠 Why this happens
HR professionals are often hired into roles that don't match their ambitions. Life Sciences employees in R&D roles have strong alignment between education and work — they tend to stay.
⚡ Business action / model decision
Review role-to-education alignment during hiring. Ensure HR-educated employees have career paths into HRBP, Talent, and Analytics roles — not just administrative functions.

📊 What the chart shows
Overtime=Yes + Low Job Satisfaction = highest attrition. Overtime=No + High Satisfaction = lowest. The cells show exact rates from ~8% to 49%+.
🧠 Why this happens
This 2×2 style matrix is a powerful management tool. The top-right cell (overtime + low satisfaction) represents employees with every reason to leave and no reason to stay. They are actively searching.

Pull a list of employees with OverTime=Yes AND JobSatisfaction ≤ 2. These individuals need immediate manager 1:1s. This segment has ~49% attrition probability.
21
21. Commute Distance — Long Commutes Double Attrition WELLBEING
📊 What the chart shows
Employees within 5km: ~12% attrition. 21-30km away: ~22% attrition. Almost 2× the rate.
🧠 Why this happens
Long commutes consume discretionary time, cause daily stress, and reduce recovery time between workdays. Remote work options eliminate this as a risk factor entirely.
⚡ Business action / model decision
Offer flexible work / remote options especially for employees commuting 20+ km. Location should factor into recruitment decisions for office-mandatory roles.

22. Job Hopping History — Past Behaviour Predicts Future HIRING SIGNAL
📊 What the chart shows
Employees who previously worked at 7-9 companies have 30-40%+ attrition. Those who've stayed at 1-2 companies: much lower risk.
🧠 Why this happens
Job hopping is a behavioural signal — some people are serial movers by personality or necessity. It is not always negative (they bring diverse experience) but is a valid attrition risk factor.
⚡ Business action / model decision
Use this as a screening consideration in hiring for critical roles (not a dealbreaker). For existing employees, focus retention resources on those with 5+ previous companies.

23. Salary Boxplot by Role — Within-Role Pay Gaps PAY EQUITY
📊 What the chart shows
For every job role, leavers (red) earn less than stayers (blue). The gap is especially visible for Sales Executives and Research Scientists.
🧠 Why this happens
Even controlling for role, lower-paid employees leave more. This suggests it is not just about what role someone is in — it's about whether they feel fairly compensated within that role.
⚡ Business action / model decision
Pay equity review within each job role. Identify the bottom 20% earners in each role. Prioritise pay adjustments for high-risk roles (Sales Rep, Lab Technician).
